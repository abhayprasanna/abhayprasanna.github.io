A Song to David

Christopher Smart (1722–1771) · published 1763 (excerpt)
Smart composed much of A Song to David during or immediately after his confinement in Mr. Potter's private madhouse and later St Luke's Hospital for Lunatics in London (roughly 1757–1763), where he was committed for what contemporaries called "religious mania" — a compulsion to pray aloud in public and summon strangers to kneel with him in the street. Samuel Johnson, his friend, drily remarked, "I did not think he ought to be shut up. His infirmities were not noxious to society. He insisted on people praying with him; and I'd as lief pray with Kit Smart as anyone else." The 516-line Pindaric hymn, built on elaborate numerological scaffolding, was once dismissed as the ravings of a madman and is now regarded as one of the great devotional poems in English. Sweet is the dew that falls betimes, And drops upon the leafy limes; Sweet Hermon's fragrant air: Sweet is the lily's silver bell, And sweet the wakeful tapers smell That watch for early pray'r.
Sweet the young nurse with love intense, Which smiles o'er sleeping innocence; Sweet when the lost arrive: Sweet the musician's ardour beats, While his vague mind's in quest of sweets, The choicest flow'rs to hive.
Sweeter in all the strains of love, The language of thy turtle dove, Pair'd to thy swelling chord; Sweeter with ev'ry grace endu'd, The glory of thy gratitude, Respir'd unto the Lord.
Strong is the horse upon his speed; Strong in pursuit the rapid glede, Which makes at once his game: Strong the tall ostrich on the ground; Strong thro' the turbulent profound Shoots xiphias to his aim.
Strong is the lion—like a coal His eye-ball—like a bastion's mole His chest against the foes: Strong, the gier-eagle on his sail, Strong against tide, th' enormous whale Emerges as he goes.
But stronger still, in earth and air, And in the sea, the man of pray'r; And far beneath the tide; And in the seat to faith assign'd, Where ask is have, where seek is find, Where knock is open wide.
Beauteous the fleet before the gale; Beauteous the multitudes in mail, Rank'd arms and crested heads: Beauteous the garden's umbrage mild, Walk, water, meditated wild, And all the bloomy beds.
Beauteous the moon full on the lawn; And beauteous, when the veil's withdrawn, The virgin to her spouse: Beauteous the temple deck'd and fill'd, When to the heav'n of heav'ns they build Their heart-directed vows.
Beauteous, yea beauteous more than these, The shepherd king upon his knees, For his momentous trust; With wish of infinite conceit, For man, beast, mute, the small and great, And prostrate dust to dust.
Precious the bounteous widow's mite; And precious, for extreme delight, The largess from the churl: Precious the ruby's blushing blaze, And alba's blest imperial rays, And pure cerulean pearl.
Precious the penitential tear; And precious is the sigh sincere, Acceptable to God: And precious are the winning flow'rs, In gladsome Israel's feast of bow'rs, Bound on the hallow'd sod.
More precious that diviner part Of David, ev'n the Lord's own heart, Great, beautiful, and new: In all things where it was intent, In all extremes, in each event, Proof—answ'ring true to true.
Glorious the sun in mid career; Glorious th' assembled fires appear; Glorious the comet's train: Glorious the trumpet and alarm; Glorious th' almighty stretch'd-out arm; Glorious th' enraptur'd main:
Glorious the northern lights a-stream; Glorious the song, when God's the theme; Glorious the thunder's roar: Glorious hosanna from the den; Glorious the catholic amen; Glorious the martyr's gore:
Glorious—more glorious is the crown Of Him that brought salvation down By meekness, call'd thy Son; Thou that stupendous truth believ'd, And now the matchless deed's achiev'd, Determin'd, dar'd, and done.

Smart's method is litany: he piles adjectives (Sweet, Strong, Beauteous, Precious, Glorious) in mathematically ordered groups, each culminating in a stanza that breaks the pattern upward toward God. The architecture is itself a form of prayer.

Opening litany

The "Sweet" sequence begins

Smart opens the first of five anaphoric movements. "Sweet" governs six stanzas. The tempo is domestic — dew, limes, lilies, candles — before climbing toward prayer. Note the synaesthesia: the smell of a taper, the sound of a bell, all folded into "sweetness."

"Sweet" still carried theological weight in Smart's era — cf. Psalm 34:8, "O taste and see that the Lord is good."

Speculation

The "vague mind" as self-portrait

The "musician's ardour" searching with a "vague mind" for "sweets to hive" reads uncomfortably like Smart's account of his own compositional state in the asylum. Contemporaries reported he wrote Jubilate Agno by scratching lines into the wainscoting with a key. The bee-gathering metaphor is classical (Seneca, Horace) but here it feels personal: mania as pollination.

Scripture

Song of Songs register

The "turtle dove" and "swelling chord" import the erotic-devotional idiom of Canticles. Smart is licensing an intimate, almost bridal relation to God — precisely the kind of speech that got him institutionalized when uttered in public spaces.

Bestiary

"Strong" sequence — the natural world

Smart shifts from the domestic to the wild. The glede (kite), ostrich, and xiphias (swordfish) form a deliberately eclectic bestiary, mixing KJV marginalia with classical zoology. Smart's Jubilate Agno contains hundreds of such verses, each beginning "Let" or "For," praising individual creatures — including his famous cat Jeoffry.

Military + biblical

Lion, vulture, whale

The lion (Judah), gier-eagle (Levitical clean-list), and whale (Jonah, Leviathan) triangulate a biblical cosmos of strength. "Bastion's mole" drags in the vocabulary of 18th-century military engineering — Smart was writing during the Seven Years' War (1756–1763), and martial imagery was everywhere.

Pivot

"The man of pray'r" outranks nature

The turning stanza of the "Strong" movement. All of creation's power is subordinated to the praying human — a direct inversion of the physico-theological pieties of Smart's day, which treated nature as evidence for God. Here prayer itself is the strongest natural force. Given Smart's confinement, this is not abstract theology; it is his personal manifesto. His "infirmity" was compulsive public prayer.

"Beauteous" sequence

From war to gardens

Smart juxtaposes the beauty of a battle fleet and ranked soldiery with the tended garden — both are "meditated wild," designed by human art to resemble natural order. The Augustan aesthetic (Pope's Twickenham, Shenstone's Leasowes) is being baptized into devotion.

Hebraism

The temple and the bride

"Heaven of heavens" is a calque from biblical Hebrew, signaling Smart's immersion in the original languages — he was translating the Psalms into English verse during and after his confinement. The virgin-to-spouse image is orthodox typology (Christ and Church) but rendered with unusual intimacy.

David arrives

The shepherd king on his knees

The poem's title subject finally appears in the "Beauteous" climax. David — warrior, adulterer, poet, penitent — is Smart's avatar: the divinely chosen singer whose gift is inseparable from his failures. Smart identified with David's composition of the Psalms under affliction, an affliction he interpreted his own madness as mirroring.

"Precious" sequence

Widow's mite to ruby

The yoking of the widow's two coins to imperial gemstones is characteristic Smart: valuation reversed by grace. The churl's unexpected largesse may be autobiographical — during his asylum years Smart depended on the erratic generosity of unlikely patrons.

Liturgy

Tears, sighs, Sukkot

Smart folds Christian penitential practice (the "sigh sincere") into Jewish festival observance (Sukkot, the Feast of Booths). Unusual for a mid-18th-century Anglican: Smart treats Hebrew liturgy as ongoing revelation, not as superseded type. His Psalm translations carry the same conviction.

David's heart

"A man after mine own heart"

Echoes 1 Samuel 13:14 and Acts 13:22. "Proof—answ'ring true to true" invokes logical proof: David's interior matches God's interior point-for-point. Smart's compressed forensic diction here anticipates Hopkins by a century.

"Glorious" climax begins

Cosmos as choir

The final anaphora ascends: sun, stars, comets, trumpet, God's arm, the enraptured sea. Smart lived through Halley's-Comet fervor (the 1759 return was the first predicted comet in history) and the cosmic imagery reflects a moment when astronomy and doxology were not yet estranged.

Speculation

"Hosanna from the den"

Read biographically, the "den" is the asylum; the "hosanna" is Smart's own compulsive praise. "Catholic amen" insists — against the Protestant suspicion that would have him locked up — that his voice belongs to the universal Church. Smart defends his sanity by enlarging the definition of worship until it contains him.

Coda

Determin'd, dar'd, and done

The full Song to David ends on this three-word drumbeat. Each verb marks a stage: divine resolution, incarnate risk, completed act. For Smart, just released from seven years of confinement and not yet fallen into the debtor's prison where he would die, it was also a personal creed — the poem itself had been determined, dared, and finally done. Robert Browning later called the Song the one instance in English poetry of a writer who "pierced the screen" between ordinary and visionary speech.
